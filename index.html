Project Greenhouse

Introduction

For our semester project, our team of six created Project Greenhouse, a 3D puzzle platformer. With a time limit of three months our goal was to make a short prototype that conveyed the vision of the game. We used Unreal Engine for the programming, Procreate and Photoshop for 2D Assets, and Blender for making custom animations and 3D assets.

My Tasks

At the start of the project, I was mostly responsible for programming tasks. I mainly focused on the camera and movement mechanics. I also worked on smaller functions and troubleshooting. Additionally, I took on implementing animations and setting up the player character in the game engine. Since I also have experience in art and illustration, I contributed to the artistic tasks as well. Toward the end of the project, I created textures and illustrations to visually support the storytelling.

Challenges

Like with any project, our team needed some time to get started. We had to discuss the main idea of the project a lot. It quickly became clear that our original concept wasn’t possible within the given timeframe. However, it was unclear how exactly we would adjust the project instead. Initially, we planned to focus a lot on the story of the game. But since our main goal was to create a fun product, we shifted our focus to game mechanics and game design. By focusing more on the technical aspects, we lost a clear and shared goal. As mentioned above, our biggest issue was "scope creep." This term refers to when a project’s requirements or goals grow beyond the original plan. We knew that we wanted to make a fairly complex game, but in the first few weeks, we had too many ideas for the story and mechanics. We wouldn't have had enough time to do all of them. It’s important to set clear boundaries at the start of a project and keep realistic expectations.

Key Learnings

In summary, working on this project was a very valuable learning experience. Throughout the project, I improved my skills in programming, animation, technical art, illustration, and design. But even more importantly, I gained more experience with teamwork and communication. Good and consistent communication often doesn’t seem so important in the initial project planning phase. It’s easy to get lost in technical details, possible implementations, and ambitions. But enjoyable and productive work depends heavily on good teamwork. This not only helped us work much more efficiently but also created a more pleasant work environment.

The game can be found here. The project was also featured on our University Website.

Salary Estimator

Overview

In this project I developed a machine learning model to estimate the average salary for Software Developers based on factors like experience, education, and country of employment. The goal was to create a tool to centralize salary information, helping users avoid the hassle of researching across multiple sources.

Workflow & Technologies

The project used the Stack Overflow Developer Survey data. After data cleaning and preprocessing to handle missing values, categorical encoding, and standardization, three models were trained and evaluated: Linear Regression, Decision Tree Regressor, and Random Forest Regressor. The final model pipeline, consisted of both preprocessing and the model. It was created and tuned through grid search. It was deployed as a web app using Streamlit.

Key Learnings

The project highlighted the importance of careful data cleaning and preprocessing. Both missing and unbalanced that created problems which will need to be addressed better in the future. Splitting the data and avoiding data leakage during preprocessing is also of importance. Additionally, the project reinforced the need for well thought out imputation techniques to improve model accuracy. Complex models might require a more in depth hyper parameter tuning. Given the time, an iterative approach to model training is beneficial.

Crystal Defense - A Tower Defense VR Game

Introduction

Our team of five set out to create a VR game in the span three months. One of the main focus points of the project was including modern VR technology. For that we made us of a VR treadmill. We aimed to build a visually immersive and engaging tower defense game.

Scope and Technologies

With limited time, we decided on a VR tower defense game to balance scalability with quality. Our goal was a rich visual experience that worked well in VR. We used Unreal Engine and Visual Studio for core development, with Blender and Procreate to create custom assets and textures. Our development involved both C++ programming and Unreal’s Blueprint visual scripting.

My Responsibilities

I developed of the building system, enabling players to place towers within the world. I also designed enemy models and integrated animations to make interactions feel more dynamic. Additionally, I created textures and visual effects to enhance the game's atmosphere.

Challenges and Solutions

Two main challenges emerged during development. First, optimizing performance for VR was a large focus. For this purpose we tried controlling polygon counts and effects, so that lag and techinical difficulties woudn't hinder the game play. Second, designing a user interface for VR was challenging. The UI needed to move with the player for easy interaction. Finding a good distance and giving good interaction feedback was a big challenge. While our solutions worked, we saw how refining UI design could further improve gameplay.

Impact of our Game

Out team was asked to showcase the game both at the GG Bavaria Convention in Munich and our university’s career day. Our professor chose our team to represent the university’s game development program. We presented in the GameLab, a space for student projects equipped with the latest hardware. To see more of our project and other student work, visit our university’s website here.

Portfolio Website

Introduction

Embarking on my very first web development project, I aimed to create a portfolio website that would not only be a learning experience but also make use of newer technologies. Instead of taking the easy route of copying existing portfolio templates, I wanted to make something unique. With my love for both 2D and 3D art, I decided to dive into Three.js to bring 3D models into the mix.

Designing with Stylized 3D

My design process kicked off with me getting my hands dirty in Blender. I created a stylized room as the centerpiece for the website. This room set the tone for the entire design, especially when it came to colors. I wanted it to look good, even as a standalone art piece. So, I had two goals in mind: to make it visually appealing with a pleasing color scheme, and to keep the model's poly count low to ensure smooth performance without sacrificing the artistic flair.

Learning the Basics

Being a total newbie in web development, I had to start from scratch and learn HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. My aim was to grasp the fundamentals and figure out one of the many approaches to web development. But the most important thing was to make it work in the end! Despite the challenges, it was a fun experience, and I'm eager to continue learning and exploring more.

Utilizing Frameworks and Tools

Of course, I couldn't go completely framework-free and tackle everything on my own, since that would have taken far too long. So, I decided to make use of Three.js to handle all the 3D assets, and I took advantage of GSAP's scrolltrigger feature to add camera and room animations.

Responsive Design Challenges

After working on the website on the side for some time, I thought everything was going smoothly. But when I resized the window to a mobile format, I discovered that the website layout faltered when viewed on different devices. It took some tweaking and troubleshooting, especially with the 3D aspects of the website, to make the website look decent enough on both desktop and mobile devices.
